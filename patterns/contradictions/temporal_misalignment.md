# Temporal Misalignment

This pattern describes a structural contradiction caused by incompatible or
misaligned temporal assumptions within the same material.

Decisions, rules, or claims rely on different timelines, update cycles,
or notions of persistence without reconciliation.

---

## Description

A **temporal misalignment** exists when:

- Parts of the material assume different time horizons
- Update or revision cycles are incompatible
- Past, present, and future states are conflated
- Validity depends on timing that is not explicit

The contradiction arises because material cannot be simultaneously valid
across the assumed temporal frames.

---

## Typical structure

This pattern commonly appears when material:

- Treats snapshots as stable states
- Applies historical assumptions to current conditions
- Mixes real-time and delayed feedback loops
- Assumes permanence where decay or drift exists
- Ignores versioning or lifecycle boundaries

---

## Indicators

Common indicators include:

- Conclusions that were valid only at creation time
- Rules that fail after environmental change
- Assumptions of static context in dynamic systems
- Ambiguity about when claims hold

---

## Relationship to failure modes

Temporal misalignment often contributes to:

- Epistemic failure (outdated justification)
- Contextual failure (invalid assumptions)
- Institutional failure (lagging governance)

---

## Notes

Temporal misalignment is often invisible until failure occurs,
because time-based assumptions degrade silently.
