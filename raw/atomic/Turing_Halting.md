# Turing’s Halting Theorem

## Core Result
Turing’s halting theorem proves that there is no general algorithm that can decide, for every program and input, whether the program halts. This is a result about computability, not about knowledge in general.

## Frequent Overinterpretation
It is often claimed that the halting theorem shows there are problems no mind could ever solve, or that human intuition transcends computation.

## Where the Interpretive Transfer Occurs
The theorem limits algorithmic decision procedures, not reasoning, insight, or informal problem-solving. Moving from “no algorithm” to “no possible knowledge” introduces an epistemic claim not present in the theorem.

## Philosophical Residue
The theorem raises legitimate questions about the scope of mechanized reasoning, but answers to those questions require additional assumptions about minds and cognition.

## Why the Misinterpretation Is Tempting

The halting theorem is often misread because it targets a notion—mechanical procedure—that is easily conflated with reasoning in general. In everyday and mathematical practice, problem-solving involves heuristics, partial insights, and context-sensitive judgments. It is therefore tempting to contrast these informal capacities with the rigidity of algorithms.

This contrast, however, already shifts the level of analysis. Turing’s theorem concerns decision procedures that operate uniformly and without insight. It does not describe, limit, or analyze informal reasoning itself. Treating the absence of a general algorithm as evidence of a cognitive boundary confuses a constraint on formal procedures with a claim about epistemic possibility.

The intuitive force of the misinterpretation stems from projecting features of human problem-solving onto the notion of computation, and then reading the resulting mismatch as a metaphysical conclusion. Once this projection is removed, the theorem retains its full technical force without supporting claims about the nature or limits of human understanding.
