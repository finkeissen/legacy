# Scope Collapse

This pattern describes a structural contradiction
caused by the collapse or uncontrolled expansion
of a material’s scope of validity.

Claims, rules, or conclusions are applied beyond
the conditions under which they were formed,
without acknowledgment or constraint.

---

## Description

A **scope collapse** exists when:

- Material is developed under a limited scope
- That scope is later expanded, merged, or ignored
- The original constraints are not re-evaluated
- Conclusions are treated as universally applicable

The contradiction arises because assumptions
valid in one scope do not hold in another.

---

## Typical structure

This pattern commonly appears when material:

- Moves from local to global claims without justification
- Treats examples as general rules
- Applies exploratory results as operational guidance
- Extends conclusions across domains, scales, or contexts
- Collapses distinctions between “in this case” and “in general”

The transition is usually implicit,
making the scope shift difficult to detect.

---

## Indicators

Common indicators include:

- Gradual broadening of language (“some” → “most” → “all”)
- Removal of qualifiers without explanation
- Conclusions that require stronger assumptions
  than those originally stated
- Tension between stated limits and applied use

---

## What this pattern is not

This pattern does **not** describe:

- Explicit generalization with stated assumptions
- Deliberate scope testing or stress analysis
- Clearly bounded extrapolation
- Separate treatments of different scopes

The issue is not ambition,
but *unacknowledged scope change*.

---

## Relationship to failure modes

Scope collapse often contributes to:

- Epistemic failure (invalid generalization)
- Contextual failure (misapplied assumptions)
- Institutional failure (overreach beyond mandate)

It may also interact with other contradiction patterns.

---

## Usage

This pattern may be referenced in:

- `case.md` to explain why conclusions no longer hold
- Failure mode documentation as a contributing mechanism
- Reviews of legacy material reused outside its original context

It does not constitute a STOP decision on its own.

---

## Notes

Scope collapse is especially common
when material transitions from exploratory,
theoretical, or advisory contexts
into operational or decision-making use.

Once collapsed, scope is difficult to restore
without revisiting original assumptions in detail.
