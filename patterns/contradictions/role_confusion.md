# Role Confusion

This pattern describes a structural contradiction
caused by the mixing or shifting of roles, perspectives,
or authorities within the same material.

Statements, rules, or decisions are made as if
they originate from a single, coherent role,
when in fact multiple incompatible roles are in play.

---

## Description

A **role confusion** exists when:

- Different roles are required to interpret the material
- Those roles carry incompatible responsibilities or authority
- The material does not distinguish between them
- Conclusions depend on silently switching perspectives

The contradiction arises because claims that are valid
from one role become invalid or unsafe when treated
as if they applied from another.

---

## Typical structure

This pattern commonly appears when material:

- Alternates between descriptive and prescriptive voice
- Mixes observer, participant, and authority perspectives
- Treats analysis as decision, or decision as analysis
- Assumes responsibility without mandate
- Uses “we”, “one”, or “the system” ambiguously

Because role shifts are implicit,
the resulting contradictions are often subtle.

---

## Indicators

Common indicators include:

- Statements that change meaning
  depending on who is assumed to be speaking
- Rules that presuppose authority not previously established
- Analyses that quietly turn into instructions
- Accountability that cannot be clearly assigned

---

## What this pattern is not

This pattern does **not** describe:

- Explicit role definitions with clear boundaries
- Multi-perspective analysis that is clearly labeled
- Deliberate handoffs between roles
- Scenario-based role exploration

The defining feature is *implicit role switching*.

---

## Relationship to failure modes

Role confusion frequently contributes to:

- Institutional failure (unclear authority or responsibility)
- Epistemic failure (confused standards of validity)
- Contextual failure (misapplied perspective)

It often interacts with scope collapse
and implicit assumption conflicts.

---

## Usage

This pattern may be referenced in:

- `case.md` to explain authority or responsibility breakdown
- Failure mode documentation as a contributing mechanism
- Reviews of material crossing organizational boundaries

It must not be used as a standalone STOP criterion.

---

## Notes

Role confusion is particularly dangerous
when material moves from analysis into action.

Once roles are blurred,
errors tend to propagate without clear ownership,
making correction difficult or impossible.
