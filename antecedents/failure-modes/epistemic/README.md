# Epistemic Failure Mode

This failure mode applies when material is epistemically unstable:
claims cannot be reliably justified, evaluated, or bounded
within a coherent knowledge framework.

The failure concerns how knowledge is formed, validated, or applied —
not merely whether individual statements are true or false.

---

## Definition

An **epistemic failure** exists when material lacks
a stable basis for knowing, validating, or trusting its own claims.

This includes breakdowns in justification, scope, inference,
or evidentiary grounding that make the material unsafe for reuse,
even if parts of it appear plausible or correct.

---

## Typical triggers

This failure mode is commonly triggered when:

- Claims lack explicit or stable justification
- Evidence is selectively applied or inconsistently weighted
- Inference rules shift without acknowledgment
- Scope of validity is undefined or silently expanded
- Confidence exceeds what the available support can sustain
- Knowledge sources are mixed without reconciliation
  (e.g. intuition, data, authority, speculation)

---

## What this mode does *not* cover

Epistemic failure does **not** apply when:

- The issue is a single factual error with otherwise sound grounding
- The material is incomplete but epistemically cautious
- Uncertainty is explicitly acknowledged and bounded
- Failures arise primarily from interaction or scale effects

Those cases may fall under other failure modes.

---

## Recognition heuristic

Ask:

- How does this material know what it claims to know?
- Are the standards of justification consistent throughout?
- Is the scope of validity explicit and defensible?

If these questions cannot be answered coherently,
this failure mode likely applies.

---

## Notes

Epistemic failure is often subtle.

Material may appear rigorous while relying on
unstated assumptions, shifting standards,
or implicit authority that collapses under scrutiny.
