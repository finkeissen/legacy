# Banach–Tarski Theorem

## Core Result
The Banach–Tarski theorem shows that, assuming the axiom of choice, a solid sphere can be decomposed and reassembled into two spheres of the same size.

## Frequent Overinterpretation
It is often taken as showing that mathematics contradicts physical reality.

## Where the Interpretive Transfer Occurs
The theorem concerns abstract set-theoretic constructions, not physical possibility.

## Philosophical Residue
The result raises questions about idealization and applicability, not about inconsistency.

## Why the Misinterpretation Is Tempting

The Banach–Tarski theorem strongly violates everyday geometric and physical intuition. Volume, congruence, and decomposition are concepts learned through interaction with physical objects, where conservation principles implicitly apply. It is therefore natural to read the theorem as a statement about what could, in principle, be done with physical matter.

This intuition silently imports empirical constraints into a purely set-theoretic context. The theorem operates entirely within an abstract framework that allows non-measurable sets and idealized decompositions. None of these constructions are required to correspond to physically realizable operations.

The apparent contradiction arises when ideal mathematical existence is conflated with physical possibility. Once this projection is removed, the theorem no longer appears paradoxical. It does not challenge physical reality; it exposes the gap between mathematical idealization and empirical applicability.
