# Decision Theory — Fillet

## 1. Core Formal Structure
Decision theory formalizes choice under uncertainty using:
- preferences (often via utility functions),
- beliefs (often via probabilities),
- decision rules (e.g., expected utility maximization),
- constraints (information, time, resources).

Bayesian decision theory combines probabilistic updating with utility-based choice, yielding explicit prescriptions conditional on a model.

## 2. Standard Interpretation
“Rationality” is often read as:
- a universal norm for agents,
- a property of minds,
- or a guarantee of correctness.
Expected utility is treated as “what one should do,” independent of context and modeling assumptions.

## 3. Illicit Upgrades
- **Representation theorem → moral law**: treating conditions under which preferences can be represented as axioms of rationality that everyone must follow.
- **Probability as belief → probability as truth**: sliding from credence to fact without an empirical interface.
- **Optimal under model → optimal in reality**: ignoring model misspecification and objective function choice.
- **Normative prescription → descriptive psychology**: assuming humans (or institutions) actually behave as the theory prescribes.

## 4. Failure Modes
- Hidden utility functions: importing values implicitly, then treating results as value-free.
- “Rational agent” reification: treating an idealized agent as an explanatory entity.
- Confusing calibration of beliefs with correctness of world-models.
- Overconfidence in optimization under severe uncertainty.

## 5. What Survives
- Decision theory as a **conditional calculus**: “if your goals and beliefs are these, then…”.
- Explicit separation of: objectives, constraints, beliefs, and decision rules.
- Robustness methods (satisficing, minimax regret, Bayesian model averaging) as legitimate extensions.

## 6. How It Reconnects
- Connects as: **Policy Layer** on top of models in the Matrix.
- Requires explicit declaration of: utility/objective, belief model, evidence/update rule, and loss of misspecification.
- Guardrail: do not infer “human rationality” or “moral truth” from representation theorems.
