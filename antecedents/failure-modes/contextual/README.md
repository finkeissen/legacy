# Contextual Failure Mode

This failure mode applies when material is rendered inadmissible
by changes, absences, or misalignment in the context
required for its validity.

The failure arises not from the internal structure alone,
but from dependence on conditions that are no longer present,
stable, or knowable.

---

## Definition

A **contextual failure** exists when material:

- Depends on specific environmental, situational, or background conditions
- Does not make those conditions explicit or enforceable
- Is reused outside the context that made it coherent or safe
- Cannot be evaluated independently of missing or shifted context

The material may be internally consistent,
yet inadmissible due to contextual drift or loss.

---

## Typical triggers

This failure mode is commonly triggered when:

- Material assumes background knowledge that is no longer shared
- Preconditions are implicit, outdated, or unverifiable
- Operational context has shifted without revision of assumptions
- Dependencies on external systems or states are unstated
- Local conventions are treated as universal defaults

---

## What this mode does *not* cover

Contextual failure does **not** apply when:

- The issue is primarily institutional (roles, authority, governance)
- The failure is epistemic (justification, inference, evidence)
- Context is explicit, bounded, and preserved
- Material remains valid across reasonable context variation

Those cases may fall under other failure modes.

---

## Recognition heuristic

Ask:

- What conditions must hold for this material to be valid or safe?
- Are those conditions explicit, stable, and reproducible?
- Would the material still function if the surrounding context changed?

If the answers are unclear or negative,
this failure mode likely applies.

---

## Notes

Contextual failure often appears benign,
because context is treated as “obvious” or “given”.

As material ages or moves,
implicit context becomes the primary source of instability.
