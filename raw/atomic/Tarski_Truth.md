# Tarski’s Undefinability of Truth

## Core Result
Tarski proved that for any sufficiently expressive formal language, a truth predicate for that language cannot be defined within the language itself.

## Frequent Overinterpretation
This is often read as showing that truth is mysterious, ineffable, or forever beyond formal understanding.

## Where the Interpretive Transfer Occurs
The theorem restricts definability, not intelligibility. Moving from formal undefinability to metaphysical ineffability adds assumptions not present in the result.

## Philosophical Residue
The theorem clarifies the hierarchy of languages and metalanguages but leaves open substantive theories of truth.

## Why the Misinterpretation Is Tempting

The undefinability theorem is often misread because it touches a concept—truth—that already carries heavy philosophical weight. When a formal language fails to define its own truth predicate, it is tempting to interpret this limitation as revealing something deep or mysterious about truth itself.

This temptation arises from conflating two questions: whether truth can be *defined* within a given formal language, and whether truth can be *understood* or *used* within a broader theoretical framework. Tarski’s result addresses only the first. It shows that semantic notions cannot be fully internalized without contradiction; it does not suggest that truth transcends formal analysis altogether.

The intuitive pull toward ineffability comes from projecting semantic hierarchy into metaphysics. Once the distinction between object language and metalanguage is respected, the theorem loses its aura of mystery. It establishes a structural constraint on formal definition, not a limit on meaning or semantic understanding.
