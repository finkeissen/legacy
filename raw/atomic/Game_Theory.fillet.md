# Game Theory — Fillet

## 1. Core Formal Structure
Game theory models strategic interaction via:
- players, actions, payoffs,
- information structure,
- solution concepts (e.g., Nash equilibrium, subgame perfection),
- dynamics (learning, repeated play).

The formal content concerns existence and properties of equilibria under specified assumptions.

## 2. Standard Interpretation
Equilibrium is often treated as:
- a prediction of what will happen,
- a stable social fact,
- or a normative recommendation (“this is what rational agents should do”).

## 3. Illicit Upgrades
- **Equilibrium existence → empirical inevitability**: taking “there exists a Nash equilibrium” as “agents will converge to it.”
- **Rationality assumptions → human nature**: reading idealized best-response reasoning as psychology.
- **Payoffs → real motives**: confusing modeled utilities with actual values, ethics, or intentions.
- **Model closure → social closure**: treating the game specification as exhaustive of relevant factors.

## 4. Failure Modes
- Ignoring equilibrium selection and multiplicity.
- Treating common knowledge assumptions as realistic.
- Overfitting institutions into toy games and then “explaining” them.
- Using game language to smuggle normative judgments (e.g., “dominant strategy” as moral justification).

## 5. What Survives
- Game theory as a **scenario generator**: exploring strategic possibilities conditional on assumptions.
- Comparative statics as insight into incentive sensitivity.
- Mechanism design as engineering under explicit constraints, with transparent value choices.

## 6. How It Reconnects
- Connects as: **Interaction Models** attached to agents/institutions represented in the Matrix.
- Requires explicit metadata: information assumptions, payoff interpretation, and equilibrium/learning concept.
- Guardrail: do not infer social reality from equilibrium existence alone.
